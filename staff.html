Running the labs

Plate preparation

Plates are needed from Day 1 (fingerprint plates) and again from Day 4 (project plates) onward. Standard LB agar at 1.5%, autoclaved, poured at 60 °C, allowed to set overnight before use. Aim for at least 40 plates ready for Day 1, with another 80 for project work starting Day 4.

PCR reagent stock

Primers 27F (AGAGTTTGATCMTGGCTCAG) and 1492R (TACGGYTACCTTGTTACGACTT) at 10 µM working stock. Standard Taq master mix, dNTPs at 200 µM. Order enough for at least 60 reactions per project group, plus controls. Verify primer sequences against your lab's standard before ordering.

Gel reagents

Agarose at 1% in 1× TAE, with SYBR Safe (not ethidium bromide — this is a teaching lab). DNA ladder covering 100 bp – 3000 bp; the 1500 bp band should be clearly visible. Pre-cast gels can be prepared the morning of, but make fresh for each session to avoid degradation.

Sanger sequencing

PCR products are sent out for Sanger sequencing using the same 27F and 1492R primers. Local turnaround is typically 3–5 working days. Plan for sequencing requests to go out by Day 9 (28 May) at the latest, to ensure results return in time for Day 11 (1 June) analysis.

Grading notes

Baseline test

The baseline test on Day 1 is not graded. It exists to record where students are before the course and to measure change at the endline. The endline test is the same 15 questions; administer it blind to the baseline (do not return baseline answers before endline). Mark the endline test out of 45 (15 questions × 3 points).

Project evaluation

The project component is evaluated on: (1) sample quality and methodology choices, (2) the quality of the pipeline run — clean gels, readable Sanger traces, defensible BLAST interpretations, (3) the final presentation and Q&A. Rubrics to be circulated separately.

What to look for in student work

A strong student presentation will (a) walk through the pipeline as a chain of inferences, not as a list of techniques; (b) be honest about uncertainty (a 99% BLAST hit is not automatically a species-level identification); (c) show the intermediate checks — the gel photo, the chromatogram quality, the negative control. Reward calibrated confidence over unqualified confidence. A presentation that overclaims is weaker than one that reports carefully.

Common failure modes — what students will run into

The deep reference covers these in detail. A quick checklist for the floor:

  • Confluent fingerprint plates
    Re-streak from the densest region onto a fresh plate. Diagnostic value is preserved.
  • No PCR band
    Check the gel first (loading order, ladder visible). Then suspect: bad colony pickup, contaminated reagents, wrong annealing temperature, exhausted master mix.
  • Band in negative control
    Discard the run. The cleanup is non-negotiable — start over with fresh reagents.
  • Multiple peaks at every Sanger position
    Mixed colony. Re-streak from a single colony, re-PCR, re-sequence. Do not try to interpret a mixed trace.
  • BLAST top hits in different genera
    16S resolution failure. Report at the genus level. This is not a failure to teach — it is a real epistemic limit students should understand.
